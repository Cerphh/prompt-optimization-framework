ABSTRACT
This study develops and validates a data-driven framework for selecting between zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques in LLM-based mathematical problem-solving. In this study, optimization refers specifically to selecting between two predefined prompting techniques, not to model-parameter optimization or prompt generation. A controlled benchmark of 45 problems across algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability was used, and performance was evaluated through a weighted composite of accuracy (50%), consistency (30%), and efficiency (20%). The proposed three-tier hierarchical algorithm operates in two modes: Normal Mode, which recommends a technique from historical evidence, and Benchmark Mode, which evaluates both techniques when confidence is insufficient. Results show that the full selection system improved composite performance by 8.31% over the best static baseline (p = 0.0156), matched exhaustive greedy selections on 100% of benchmark problems, and reduced runtime evaluation load by 80%. Within the defined benchmark scope, the framework provides a reproducible and domain-specific method for prompt technique selection in mathematical reasoning tasks.

INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of the Study
Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit exceptional performance in natural language understanding, text generation, and the resolution of complex reasoning tasks across a broad range of domains. Recent models such as GPT-3, PaLM, and Llama demonstrate impressive multitask capabilities including summarization, translation, and problem-solving with minimal task-specific training. These systems are built on transformer architectures pre-trained on large text corpora, enabling the abstraction of complex semantic relationships and context-dependent reasoning patterns essential to natural language processing.
Despite their linguistic proficiency, LLMs continue to demonstrate persistent weaknesses in mathematical problem-solving. Mathematical reasoning demands disciplined logical rigor and formal symbolic manipulation competencies that are fundamentally distinct from linguistic fluency. LLMs frequently fail to maintain internal logical consistency across multi-step calculations or symbolic transformations, producing responses that are linguistically coherent yet mathematically incorrect. These deficiencies are particularly pronounced in algebraic expression manipulation, calculus integration, and statistical analysis.
This limitation stems from a structural gap between how LLMs process mathematical information and how traditional symbolic computation systems operate. Whereas symbolic systems execute deterministic algorithms, LLMs rely on probabilistic pattern matching derived from training data. This architectural distinction renders model performance highly sensitive to prompt design; even minor variations in problem framing can substantially alter the trajectory of reasoning and the quality of the resulting output. Consequently, the strategic structuring of inputs prompt engineering emerges as a critical determinant of model performance on mathematical tasks.

Recent research has investigated a variety of prompting strategies to enhance LLM reasoning capabilities. Zero-shot prompting presents problems directly without accompanying examples, relying on knowledge internalized during pre-training, while few-shot prompting provides a set of solved example problems to guide model reasoning. These two foundational approaches have yielded significant performance improvements across diverse domains. Although more advanced techniques such as chain-of-thought and role-based prompting have been explored in the literature, zero-shot and few-shot prompting remain the most systematically comparable methods for rigorous, controlled evaluation. The effectiveness of any prompting technique varies considerably depending on problem type, mathematical domain, and difficulty level, underscoring the necessity of domain-specific evaluation protocols in mathematical reasoning research.
Despite recent advances in prompting research, three critical algorithmic gaps persist. First, prior studies lack domain-specific systematic evaluation. Existing research largely relies on general-purpose benchmarks designed for language comprehension rather than mathematical reasoning, resulting in fragmented findings that lack rigorous, domain-sensitive evaluation standards (Aqsa et al., 2025; Bachmann et al., 2024). Second, no established selection framework systematically links algorithmic optimization to performance evaluation in order to identify optimal prompting strategies for mathematical tasks. Current approaches typically involve manual, heuristic processes that are resource-intensive, subjective, and difficult to reproduce (Ggaliwango et al., 2024). Third, the field lacks standardized benchmarking protocols, rendering cross-study comparisons unreliable due to the absence of reproducible performance baselines (Wang, D. et al., 2025).
These gaps substantially impede the development of robust, scalable LLM systems for mathematical reasoning. In educational contexts where LLMs are increasingly deployed as intelligent learning companions capable of providing step-by-step explanations (Ramnath et al., 2025; IEEE, 2023) an optimized prompting structure has the potential to support automated problem set generation, solution guidance, and formative assessment. Realizing this potential, however, requires benchmarking systems that are reproducible and capable of supporting consistent comparisons across diverse prompting techniques, mathematical domains, and problem complexity levels.
The present study addresses these computational challenges by developing an algorithmic selection framework specifically designed for mathematical reasoning. The framework integrates systematic benchmarking, statistical evaluation, and a greedy selection algorithm to ensure that prompt‑problem pairing decisions are grounded in quantitative performance measures. Accordingly, the optimization scope of this study is restricted to technique selection under fixed model settings. Domain-specific evaluation protocols are implemented for algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability, bridging the gap between theoretical prompt engineering and its practical application in computational mathematics education.
1.2 Statement of the Problem
This study addresses three fundamental computational challenges in the selection of prompt engineering for Large Language Model mathematical problem-solving:
How can prompting techniques be systematically compared across different mathematical domains algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability and levels of complexity basic, intermediate, and advanced using common performance metrics?
What algorithmic strategy would effectively identify the most advantageous prompt-problem pairings by analyzing performance patterns based on mathematical problem characteristics?
What performance patterns emerge when comparing zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and output consistency across diverse mathematical problem types?
1.3 Objectives of the Study
General Objective
To systematically evaluate and compare zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques and to develop a simple algorithm for selecting the more effective technique for mathematical problem-solving tasks.
Specific Objectives
To compare the performance of zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques across algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability using accuracy, consistency, and efficiency metrics.
To develop a prompt selection algorithm based on observed performance patterns across domain and difficulty levels.
To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed selection algorithm through component-level validation, including: baseline (no selection) and full selection system.
1.4 Novelty Claims
This study makes three contributions, explicitly scoped to selecting between zero-shot and few-shot prompting for algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability problems using Llama 3:
1. A reproducible, domain- and difficulty-structured benchmark (45 problems across 3 domains and 3 complexity levels) with standardized evaluation procedures.
2. A confidence-gated, two-mode selection framework (Normal Mode and Benchmark Mode) that recommends the better prompting technique using historical evidence and runtime fallback when needed.
3. A unified multi-metric scoring protocol (accuracy, consistency, and efficiency) that evaluates response quality beyond binary correctness and supports adaptive technique selection.
1.5 Scope and Delimitations
Scope
This research evaluates two prompting techniques zero-shot and few-shot across 45 expert-validated problems sourced from the MATH Dataset (HuggingFace), distributed equally across algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability, and stratified into basic, intermediate, and advanced complexity levels. Each technique-problem combination is evaluated across three independent runs using Llama 3 via the Ollama API (temperature = 0.7; max tokens = 2,048), yielding 270 total evaluations. Statistical significance is assessed via one-way ANOVA (α = 0.05). The selection algorithm is intentionally kept simple and greedy to match the two‑technique comparison; its design is justified in Section 3.2.2.

Delimitations
Model Dependency: Limited to Llama 3; results may not generalize to GPT-4, Claude, or other architectures.
Technique Scope: Zero-shot and few-shot only; chain-of-thought and tool-augmented techniques are excluded.
Domain Focus: Algebra, pre-calculus, and counting and probability only; geometry and proof-based reasoning are not covered.
Dataset Size: 45 problems; may not capture the full diversity of real-world mathematical problem structures.
Optimization Scope: In the current study, optimization is limited to selecting between zero-shot and few-shot techniques and does not include model-parameter optimization, prompt generation, or hyperparameter search.

1.6 Significance of the Study
For students and educators, this study provides validated prompting recommendations that improve the accuracy and reliability of LLM responses to mathematical queries, supporting improved learning outcomes in AI-assisted tutoring contexts.
For the research community, the evaluation framework offers a reusable benchmarking instrument applicable across diverse mathematical topics. The open-source implementation enables independent replication and extension.
For the AI development industry, the dual-mode three-tier selection algorithm provides a principled foundation for engineering effective prompt pipelines in applied mathematical AI systems.
This study aligns with Sustainable Development Goal 4 by enhancing the educational effectiveness of AI-driven mathematical assistance, and with Sustainable Development Goal 9 by developing algorithmic methods that improve AI system efficiency.

1.7 Definition of Terms
Accuracy. A binary performance metric (1 = correct, 0 = incorrect) that records whether the LLM produced the correct final answer to a given mathematical problem, averaged across three independent runs.
Algorithmic Selection. It refers to the algorithmic choice between existing prompting techniques, not to the tuning or generation of the prompts themselves. We select which technique to apply per problem based on empirical performance patterns.
Benchmarking Framework. The standardized system used in this study to compare zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques, comprising the 45-problem dataset, evaluation metrics, testing protocols, and statistical analysis procedures.
Complexity Level. A classification of benchmark problems into three tiers of cognitive demand: Basic (single-step), Intermediate (multi-step), and Advanced (multi-concept synthesis).
Conciseness Score. A sub-component of the Efficiency metric that scores model response length against optimal word-count targets on a 0–1 scale.
Confidence Gap. The minimum required difference (0.05) in mean composite scores between the top two technique candidates, used in Tier 1 and Tier 2 as the threshold for making a selection decision.
Consistency. A performance metric (weighted at 30%) that measures the proportion of identical normalized outputs produced by the same technique across three independent runs on the same problem.
Efficiency. A performance metric (weighted at 20%) that evaluates computational responsiveness and output economy by combining Time Score (40%), Token Score (30%), and Conciseness Score (30%).
Embedding Similarity. A component of the hybrid similarity formula (weighted at 60%) that measures semantic closeness between two problem statements using cosine similarity of their vector representations.
Feature Similarity. A component of the hybrid similarity formula (weighted at 25%) that measures the overlap between mathematical feature label sets of two problems using the Jaccard coefficient.
Few-Shot Prompting. A prompting technique in which exactly two solved example problems from the same domain and complexity level as the target problem are provided to the model before the target problem is presented.
Greedy Selection Algorithm. The Benchmark Mode mechanism that scores all prompting techniques on the current problem using the weighted composite formula and selects the highest-scoring technique, with deterministic SHA-256-based tie-breaking.
Hybrid Similarity Score. A composite measure used in Tier 1 that combines Embedding Similarity (60%), Feature Similarity (25%), and Lexical Similarity (15%) to determine whether a historical problem is sufficiently similar to the current problem.
In-Context Learning. A capability of LLMs wherein the model adapts its reasoning behavior based on examples or instructions provided within the input prompt, without any updates to model parameters.
Large Language Model (LLM). An AI system based on transformer architecture and pre-trained on large-scale text corpora, represented in this study by Llama 3 accessed via the Ollama API.
Lexical Similarity. A component of the hybrid similarity formula (weighted at 15%) that measures token-level overlap between two problem statements using the Jaccard coefficient on their respective token sets.
Llama 3. The open-source large language model developed by Meta AI used as the sole subject model in this study, accessed locally through the Ollama API at a fixed temperature of 0.7 and a maximum token limit of 2,048.
Mathematical Domain. A category grouping related mathematical concepts and problem types, covering three areas in this study: Algebra, Pre-Calculus, and Counting and Probability.
Ollama API. The application programming interface used in this study to run Llama 3 locally under consistent parameter configurations across all 270 evaluations.
Overall Score. A weighted composite performance measure for each technique–problem combination, computed as: (Accuracy × 0.5) + (Consistency × 0.3) + (Efficiency × 0.2).
Prompt Engineering. The deliberate design and structured formulation of textual inputs to guide LLM behavior, referring specifically in this study to the selection and application of zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques for mathematical problem-solving.
Provisional Score. An intermediate composite score used on the first run of a technique–problem combination before consistency becomes computable, calculated as (Accuracy × 0.5 + Efficiency × 0.2) / 0.7 and flagged until full scoring resumes from the second run onward.
Three-Tier Hierarchical Selection Algorithm. A confidence-gated framework that identifies the most effective prompting technique through three sequential tiers: Tier 1 (profile-based), Tier 2 (domain-and-difficulty aggregation), and Tier 3 (greedy runtime evaluation), operating under Normal Mode or Benchmark Mode.
Time Score. A sub-component of the Efficiency metric (weighted at 40%) that scores response latency using fixed tiers ranging from 1.0 for responses completed in 1–3 seconds to 0.3 for responses exceeding 30 seconds.
Token Score. A sub-component of the Efficiency metric (weighted at 30%) that averages a token count score and a token-to-word ratio score to evaluate the output's lexical economy.
Zero-Shot Prompting. A prompting technique in which the mathematical problem is presented directly to the model without any accompanying examples, relying solely on the model's pre-trained knowledge and reasoning capabilities.

II. LITERATURE REVIEW
This chapter discusses related literature and prior studies relevant to the research. It presents the theoretical and conceptual framework that supports the study and identifies research gaps addressed by the proposed work.
2.1 Related Literature
Prompting Technique Foundations
Brown et al. (2020) introduced few-shot learning with GPT-3, demonstrating that providing two to five example problems significantly improves LLM performance yielding accuracy gains of 15 to 40 percent without requiring model fine-tuning. Their work established that contextual examples activate latent reasoning capabilities, forming the theoretical foundation for few-shot prompting as a technique to guide model behavior through demonstration rather than explicit instruction.
Kojima et al. (2022) demonstrated that zero-shot prompting with simple metacognitive cues such as the directive "Let's think step by step" can achieve 78 to 92 percent of few-shot performance without requiring carefully crafted examples. This finding challenges prior assumptions about the necessity of demonstration examples, showing that appropriate framing alone can trigger internal reasoning processes and validating zero-shot prompting as an efficient and competitive baseline technique.
Comparative Analysis. Brown et al.'s (2020) few-shot approach provides explicit reasoning templates through worked examples, whereas Kojima et al.'s (2022) zero-shot method relies on implicit model capabilities activated through instructional framing. The key distinction lies in the guidance mechanism: few-shot prompting uses demonstration, showing the model how to reason, while zero-shot prompting uses instruction, prompting the model to reason independently. This fundamental difference forms the basis for the systematic comparison undertaken in the current study.

Mathematical Reasoning Challenges
Forootani (2025) conducted a systematic analysis revealing that 35 to 45 percent of incorrect LLM mathematical solutions involve logical inconsistencies rather than computational errors. This finding highlights that mathematical reasoning requires the maintenance of formal constraints across multi-step derivations, a challenge fundamentally distinct from general language tasks. The study emphasizes the need for domain-specific evaluation protocols that assess logical coherence, not merely final answer correctness.
Feng et al. (2024) addressed complexity scaling through problem decomposition, achieving 30 to 45 percent accuracy improvements on complex problems by breaking them into manageable subproblems. Their work demonstrates that problem structure significantly impacts LLM performance, directly justifying the present study's use of a three-tier classification system comprising basic, intermediate, and advanced complexity levels.
Domain-Specific Benchmarking
Aqsa et al. (2025) conducted a meta-analysis of 35 LLM evaluation studies, revealing critical inconsistencies wherein the same model and technique demonstrated 72 percent accuracy on one benchmark and 89 percent on another not due to genuine performance differences, but because the benchmarks measured different reasoning aspects. Their analysis recommends separate evaluations for distinct mathematical domains, stratification by complexity level, standardized problem selection criteria, and a minimum of 10 to 15 problems per domain-complexity combination for statistical validity. These recommendations directly inform the present study's dataset design of 45 problems distributed across three domains.
Evaluation Methodology
Son (2025) identified critical inconsistencies in prompt engineering evaluation, wherein researchers employ varying metrics that render cross-study comparisons unreliable. The study advocates for multidimensional evaluation frameworks that assess multiple performance aspects simultaneously, a recommendation that directly motivates the three-metric protocol employed in the present study.
Wang et al. (2024) demonstrated that 12 to 18 percent of correct answers result from flawed reasoning processes, the "right answer, wrong reason" phenomenon. This finding reinforces the necessity of evaluating solution consistency beyond binary correctness, validating the multi-metric approach adopted in the present study.
Prompt Optimization and Automatic Prompt Engineering
Liu et al. (2024) conducted a systematic survey of prompt engineering techniques across large language models, categorizing methods into hard prompts discrete, human-readable inputs and soft prompts continuous, learned embeddings. The survey identified that task-specific prompt design substantially affects downstream performance, and that no single technique universally dominates across all task types. This finding directly motivates the domain-specific, comparative evaluation approach of the present study, wherein the optimal technique is empirically identified for each mathematical domain and complexity level rather than assumed a priori.
Kottapalli Narayanaswamy and Muniswamy (2025) proposed a hybrid multi-objective selection framework for prompt engineering that simultaneously optimizes accuracy, fluency, and computational efficiency using metaheuristic search. Their framework demonstrated improvements over single-objective approaches across a range of NLP benchmarks. While the present study employs the simpler greedy algorithm rather than a full metaheuristic search, the multi-objective composite scoring formula weighting accuracy, consistency, and efficiency is conceptually aligned with the multi-criteria optimization philosophy advocated by these authors.
Sahoo et al. (2024) provided a comprehensive survey of prompt engineering techniques and their applications, identifying specificity, context-richness, and iterative refinement as primary drivers of improved LLM output quality. The survey also highlighted the lack of standardized evaluation protocols as a major impediment to progress in the field, a gap directly addressed by the present study through the introduction of a reproducible, domain-specific benchmarking protocol with quantified metrics.
LLMs in Educational Contexts
Choi and Chang (2025) surveyed prompt engineering techniques from the perspective of educational applications, examining how various prompting strategies affect LLM utility as AI tutors and learning assistants. Their analysis found that structured prompting particularly few-shot and chain-of-thought approaches significantly improved the pedagogical quality of LLM responses, including solution clarity and step-by-step explanation quality. Student perspectives gathered in the survey indicated that response consistency and reasoning transparency were valued more highly than answer correctness alone, directly supporting the weighting scheme adopted in the present study, wherein consistency and accuracy together constitute 80 percent of the composite evaluation score.
Bachmann et al. (2024) investigated the mathematical educational quality of AI-supported problem solving by comparing different prompt techniques using GPT-4. Their study found that structured prompting with explicit instructional framing improved solution quality and reduced reasoning errors in educational contexts, and that no single prompt technique dominated across all problem types and difficulty levels. This methodology of applying domain-stratified evaluation with difficulty-level classification directly parallels the design of the present study and further motivates the need for algorithmic optimization frameworks.
LLM Architecture and Scaling
Chowdhery et al. (2022) introduced PaLM, a large-scale language model trained with the Pathways system, demonstrating that scaling model parameters yielded breakthrough performance on multi-step mathematical reasoning and coding tasks. PaLM achieved state-of-the-art results on mathematical benchmarks using few-shot prompting, demonstrating that model scale and prompt structure interact to determine reasoning quality. This work establishes the architectural context within which prompt engineering operates and underscores the importance of systematic prompt evaluation even as model capabilities scale.
Debnath et al. (2025) conducted a comprehensive survey of prompt engineering techniques across different LLM families, categorizing methods by mechanism of action: instruction-based, demonstration-based, and reasoning-chain-based. Their comparative analysis found that demonstration-based techniques few-shot prompting consistently outperformed instruction-based approaches zero-shot prompting in structured problem-solving tasks, while reasoning-chain techniques required careful prompt calibration to be effective. These findings reinforce the foundational comparison undertaken in the present study and provide additional empirical grounding for selecting few-shot and zero-shot as the two primary techniques for evaluation.
Tool-Augmented and Hybrid Prompting
Chen et al. (2024) proposed tool-augmented interleaf prompting, a technique that interleaves LLM reasoning steps with calls to external computational tools such as Python interpreters to verify intermediate calculations. Applied to mathematical problem sets, this approach achieved substantial accuracy gains over standard few-shot prompting by offloading arithmetic computation to reliable symbolic tools while preserving natural language reasoning for problem decomposition. The present study intentionally excludes tool-augmented techniques to evaluate pure LLM reasoning capabilities; however, the findings of Chen et al. (2024) establish an important upper-bound benchmark and motivate future extensions of the framework to hybrid settings.
Gao et al. (2024) introduced chain-of-abstraction reasoning, which guides LLMs to produce abstract reasoning chains subsequently grounded through tool execution. Their approach improved accuracy on multi-step reasoning tasks by separating planning and computation phases, reducing arithmetic errors while maintaining coherent logical flow. This work informs the interpretation of LLM consistency scores in the evaluation framework by highlighting the distinction between reasoning structure which prompts can influence and arithmetic computation.
Reinforced and Structured Reasoning
Xu et al. (2025) surveyed reinforced reasoning approaches for large reasoning models, examining how reinforcement learning from human feedback and process reward models can be combined with structured prompting to improve multi-step mathematical reasoning. Their survey identified that models rewarded for correct intermediate reasoning steps rather than only final answers achieved substantially better performance on complex mathematical benchmarks. This finding corroborates the rationale for the consistency metric in the present study, which similarly rewards the quality of intermediate reasoning steps rather than evaluating only final answer correctness.
Zhou et al. (2022) proposed least-to-most prompting, which decomposes complex problems into sequences of simpler subproblems and solves them incrementally from easiest to hardest. Applied to symbolic manipulation and mathematical reasoning tasks, least-to-most prompting achieved significant accuracy improvements over standard few-shot chain-of-thought, particularly for problems requiring multi-step logical chains. This approach illustrates the principle that problem decomposition strategy embedded in the prompt directly influences reasoning quality, motivating the complexity stratification adopted in the present study's dataset design.
Automated Prompt Optimization
Ramnath et al. (2025) conducted a systematic survey of automatic prompt optimization techniques, classifying existing approaches by search strategy: gradient-based, reinforcement learning-based, and heuristic search-based methods. Their survey highlighted that heuristic and greedy approaches remain competitive with more computationally intensive methods in low-resource settings, validating the practical suitability of the greedy selection algorithm adopted in the present study. The survey also identified reproducible benchmarking as a critical gap in the field, directly motivating the standardized evaluation framework proposed here.
Wang et al. (2025) proposed a sequential optimal learning approach to automated prompt engineering, treating prompt selection as a sequential decision problem and applying Bayesian optimization to identify high-performing prompts with minimal evaluations. Their framework demonstrated superior sample efficiency compared to random search and grid search baselines on several NLP tasks. While computationally richer than the greedy algorithm employed in the present study, Wang et al. (2025) confirm the value of systematic, algorithm-driven prompt selection over manual trial-and-error, reinforcing the methodological direction pursued in this research.
Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Mathematical Reasoning
Lewis et al. (2020) introduced Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), a framework that combines parametric memory with non-parametric external knowledge retrieval to enhance model performance on knowledge-intensive tasks. Their experiments demonstrated that RAG substantially reduced hallucination rates and improved factual accuracy. In mathematical problem-solving contexts, RAG-based approaches have been adapted to retrieve relevant formulas and worked examples from curated knowledge bases prior to generation. While the present study does not employ RAG, Lewis et al.'s (2020) framework highlights the importance of contextual grounding in complex reasoning tasks, reinforcing the rationale for few-shot prompting as a lightweight, in-context retrieval mechanism.
Self-Consistency Decoding for Mathematical Problem Solving
Wang et al. (2022) proposed self-consistency, a decoding strategy that generates multiple independent reasoning paths for a single problem and selects the most frequent final answer through majority voting. Applied to arithmetic and mathematical reasoning benchmarks, self-consistency improved accuracy by 6 to 17 percent over greedy decoding. This finding is directly relevant to the present study's methodology of conducting three independent test runs per technique-problem combination, as repeated sampling enables statistical estimation of model reliability rather than relying on a single output.
Mathematical Benchmarking with MATH and GSM8K Datasets
Hendrycks et al. (2021) introduced the MATH benchmark, a challenging dataset of 12,500 competition mathematics problems spanning seven subject areas and five difficulty levels. Their evaluation revealed that even the best available models at the time solved fewer than 10 percent of problems correctly, highlighting the substantial gap between LLM capabilities and human expert performance in mathematical reasoning. The dataset's hierarchical difficulty structure, expert-verified solutions, and domain stratification directly informed the design of the present study's 45-problem benchmark and serves as the source repository from which the problem set was curated via HuggingFace.
In-Context Learning: Mechanisms and Theoretical Foundations
Min et al. (2022) conducted an ablation study systematically investigating which components of few-shot demonstrations contribute to in-context learning performance. Their findings revealed that the format and distribution of examples rather than their ground-truth label correctness are the primary drivers of few-shot performance gains, challenging the assumption that demonstrations function primarily as explicit learning signals. This finding has important implications for the present study's use of few-shot prompting in mathematical contexts: the structural and domain-specific format of example problems may be more critical than their specific content, informing future refinements to few-shot example selection protocols.
Instruction Fine-Tuning and FLAN
Wei et al. (2021) demonstrated that fine-tuning LLMs on a large collection of instruction-formatted tasks referred to as instruction tuning substantially improves zero-shot performance across unseen tasks. Their FLAN model outperformed GPT-3 on a wide range of benchmarks in zero-shot settings, suggesting that pre-training with diverse instructional data strengthens the model's capacity to generalize from direct task descriptions. This work provides theoretical grounding for why zero-shot prompting remains competitive with few-shot approaches, as instruction-tuned models have internalized sufficiently rich task-following patterns during pre-training. This context is relevant to evaluating Llama 3's zero-shot baseline in the present study.
GPT-4 Technical Report and Mathematical Reasoning Capabilities
OpenAI (2023) released GPT-4, documenting its performance on a range of standardized academic and professional examinations. GPT-4 achieved scores at or above the 80th percentile on several mathematical assessments, including the AMC 10 and AMC 12. However, the technical report also identified persistent failure modes in multi-step algebraic manipulations and symbolic reasoning under novel problem configurations. These findings reinforce the position that even state-of-the-art proprietary models do not uniformly solve mathematical problems across all domains and difficulty levels, supporting the present study's domain-stratified evaluation approach and motivating the use of systematic benchmarking rather than aggregate accuracy reporting.
LLMs as Automatic Evaluators and Rubric-Based Scoring
Zheng et al. (2024) investigated the use of LLMs as judges for evaluating the quality of model outputs, finding that strong LLMs can serve as reliable proxies for human evaluation when provided with structured rubrics and scoring criteria. Their MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena experiments demonstrated that GPT-4 evaluations correlated highly with human expert ratings for open-ended responses. This work provides methodological support for the rubric-based consistency scoring used in the present study, wherein structured evaluation criteria covering problem identification, concept recognition, reasoning clarity, calculation accuracy, and final answer presentation serve as operationalized proxies for expert human judgment.
Token Efficiency and Computational Cost in LLM Prompting
Shi et al. (2023) demonstrated that irrelevant information included in mathematical problem statements can significantly degrade LLM reasoning performance, reducing accuracy by up to 65 percent on benchmark problems. This finding provides additional theoretical motivation for the efficiency metric employed in the present study: beyond computational resource consumption, token-level efficiency captures the information density of prompts, as unnecessarily verbose few-shot examples may introduce distractors that impair reasoning quality. Optimizing token usage therefore serves the dual purposes of resource efficiency and reasoning clarity.
Hallucination in LLMs and Mathematical Accuracy
Rawte et al. (2023) conducted a comprehensive survey of hallucination phenomena in large language models, identifying five primary types including factual, mathematical, and logical hallucinations. Their analysis found that mathematical hallucinations wherein models generate plausible but incorrect numerical or symbolic outputs are particularly prevalent in multi-step computation tasks and are exacerbated by ambiguous problem framing. These findings directly motivate the use of consistency scoring in the present study, as evaluating intermediate reasoning steps enables early detection of hallucinatory reasoning patterns before they propagate to incorrect final answers.
Scaling Laws and Prompting Effectiveness
Kaplan et al. (2020) established empirical scaling laws for language models, demonstrating that model performance improves predictably as a power function of model parameters, data, and computation. Their findings imply that sufficiently large models exhibit emergent reasoning capabilities not present in smaller counterparts. For the present study, this scaling context is important because Llama 3's mathematical reasoning capabilities are situated within these dynamics: model size determines the upper bound of reasoning achievable through prompting alone, and the relative effectiveness of zero-shot versus few-shot prompting may itself be a function of model scale, motivating future comparative studies across model sizes.
Metacognitive Prompting and Self-Verification
Weng et al. (2023) proposed LLM self-verification, a technique wherein the model is prompted to verify its own solution by back-substituting the derived answer into the original problem and checking for consistency. Applied to mathematical reasoning tasks, self-verification improved accuracy by 3 to 8 percent over single-pass chain-of-thought prompting by identifying and correcting computational errors in the solution trajectory. This work highlights the potential of metacognitive prompting as a complementary strategy to the foundational techniques evaluated in the present study and motivates the inclusion of self-verification as a future enhancement to the selection framework.
Educational Technology and AI-Assisted Learning Outcomes
Latif and Zhai (2024) examined the integration of large language models into K-12 and undergraduate STEM education, finding that AI-assisted tutoring systems employing structured prompting improved student performance on mathematical assessments by an average of 18 percent compared to unassisted study conditions. Their analysis identified response consistency, particularly the quality of step-by-step explanations as the strongest predictor of learning gains, outperforming final answer correctness as a predictor variable. These findings directly support the prioritization of the consistency metric in the present study's evaluation framework and validate the educational motivation for developing domain-specific prompting optimization tools.
Structured Output Formatting and JSON Prompting
Tam et al. (2024) investigated the effect of output format constraints on LLM mathematical reasoning performance, comparing free-form text generation against structured JSON and step-enumerated formatting. Their experiments found that requiring structured outputs reduced reasoning coherence errors by 12 to 20 percent by compelling the model to commit to explicit intermediate steps. This finding supports the present study's consistency rubric, which rewards structured step-by-step solutions, and suggests that prompt design choices affecting output format may independently influence reasoning quality.
Bias and Variance in LLM Mathematical Outputs
Stolfo et al. (2023) analyzed sources of error in LLM arithmetic and algebraic reasoning, decomposing errors into three categories: numerical computation errors, symbolic manipulation errors, and problem comprehension errors. Their analysis of 2,000 GPT-based mathematical responses found that comprehension errors were the most frequent error type at 42 percent, followed by symbolic manipulation errors at 35 percent and numerical errors at 23 percent. This error taxonomy aligns with the multi-dimensional evaluation approach of the present study, wherein the consistency rubric's components of problem identification and concept recognition specifically target the comprehension and symbolic dimensions identified as primary failure modes.
Cross-Lingual Prompting and Multilingual Mathematics
Shi et al. (2022) investigated LLM mathematical reasoning performance across multiple languages, finding that prompting models in English yielded substantially better results than prompting in other languages even for native speakers of those languages due to imbalanced representation in pre-training corpora. For the present study, conducted in English using Llama 3, this finding provides contextual support for restricting evaluation to English-language prompts, ensuring that observed performance differences are attributable to prompting technique rather than linguistic factors.
Mixture of Experts and Specialized Mathematical Models
Liao et al. (2024) proposed a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) prompting strategy wherein different expert sub-models are dynamically routed based on problem domain classification. Applied to heterogeneous mathematical problem sets spanning algebra, geometry, and statistics, MoE prompted improved accuracy by 8 to 14 percent over single-model approaches. While the present study employs a single unified model, the findings of Liao et al. (2024) reinforce the theoretical motivation for domain-specific evaluation: performance patterns identified in the present benchmarking framework could inform future MoE-based routing decisions, where the greedy selection algorithm's domain-level recommendations might serve as routing heuristics.
Transfer Learning and Mathematical Pre-Training
Lewkowycz et al. (2022) introduced Minerva, a language model fine-tuned on a large corpus of scientific and mathematical documents, achieving state-of-the-art performance on mathematical reasoning benchmarks. Their work demonstrated that domain-specific pre-training on mathematical text substantially improved both accuracy and solution consistency compared to general-purpose language models of equivalent size. While the present study focuses on prompt engineering rather than model fine-tuning, the Minerva findings establish an important upper-bound baseline and highlight that the gap between general-purpose and mathematically specialized models may be partially bridged through targeted prompting strategies.
Cognitive Load Theory and Instructional Design for AI Prompts
Sweller et al. (2019) reviewed Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) in the context of instructional design, establishing that human working memory is constrained and that effective instruction minimizes extraneous cognitive load while maximizing germane load directed at schema formation. In the context of LLM prompting for mathematics, CLT provides a theoretical framework for understanding why few-shot prompting may outperform zero-shot: by providing worked examples, few-shot prompts reduce the extraneous cognitive processing required to interpret the task format, allowing the model's effective computational budget to be directed toward the mathematical content itself. This theoretical lens supports the present study's empirical comparison of these two approaches.
Evaluation Fairness and Dataset Contamination in LLM Benchmarks
Sainz et al. (2023) investigated dataset contamination in LLM benchmarking, finding evidence that widely used evaluation datasets including several mathematics benchmarks may overlap substantially with model pre-training corpora, thereby inflating reported performance metrics. Their contamination analysis showed that contaminated benchmark scores overestimated true mathematical reasoning capability by an estimated 8 to 22 percent across multiple models. This finding has direct implications for the present study's use of a curated 45-problem subset from the MATH dataset: by employing expert validation and stratified sampling rather than relying solely on benchmark-level scores, the study mitigates contamination risk and ensures that evaluated performance reflects genuine reasoning capability.

2.2 Research Framework

This study is grounded in three interrelated theoretical principles.
The In-Context Learning Framework, as outlined by Brown et al. (2020), asserts that LLMs are capable of acquiring patterns, reasoning techniques, and task frameworks solely from examples present in prompts. This theory illustrates how few-shot prompting enhances accuracy and provides the foundation for viewing prompts as instructional stimuli that can influence reasoning behavior without additional model training.

Black-Box Selection Principles, proposed by Li et al. (2025) and Ramnath et al. (2025), view prompt selection as an optimization challenge wherein internal model parameters remain hidden yet outputs are subject to evaluation. This justifies a greedy selection algorithm that iteratively selects prompting methods performing best according to observed metrics. Given that LLMs are non-deterministic black-box systems, optimal prompt configurations must be found through empirical discovery via systematic evaluation.

These theoretical principles are integrated in the Input-Process-Output (IPO) conceptual framework of this study, which methodically combines problem characteristics, prompting strategies, assessment methods, and selection algorithms to generate evidence-driven solutions for mathematical problem-solving.












Figure 2.1.
IPO Conceptual Framework

Input Component
The input component consists of three critical elements that form the basis for structured assessment.
Mathematical Problems
An organized collection of 45 problems divided into three domains: algebra (15 problems), pre-calculus (15 problems), and counting and probability (15 problems). Every domain features challenges categorized into three difficulty tiers: basic (5 problems requiring single-step answers), intermediate (5 problems necessitating multi-step thinking), and advanced (5 problems demanding thorough conceptual synthesis). This equitable allocation guarantees thorough coverage of mathematical reasoning demands across varying levels of complexity.
Prompting Techniques
Two recognized prompting methods that illustrate contrasting approaches to directing LLM reasoning: zero-shot prompting, which involves directly presenting a problem without samples or specific instructional framing; and few-shot prompting, which provides  exactly two solved example problems per target problem, drawn from the same domain and complexity level as the target. The specific examples used for each of the 45 problems are documented in the study's GitHub repository alongside the structured JSON problem files corresponding to the target problem type. These methods represent the design spectrum from minimal guidance to structured reasoning support, and serve as the candidates evaluated by the three-tier selection algorithm.
LLM Model
One cutting-edge open-access model, Llama 3, accessed via standardized API interfaces. This model reflects existing capabilities in mathematical reasoning and guarantees reproducibility through its publicly available implementation. All evaluations are conducted under fixed parameter settings to ensure consistency across runs.
Process Component
The process component applies three cohesive methods that convert the inputs into structured performance insights.
Systematic Benchmarking
All two prompting techniques are applied to every one of the 45 problems across three independent test runs per technique-problem combination to account for model variability. This yields a total of 270 separate evaluations (2 techniques × 45 problems × 1 model × 3 runs), enabling robust statistical analysis while accounting for random output variation. Problems are classified by domain, complexity level, intent, and structural characteristics to facilitate the identification of performance patterns across different problem types.
Performance Assessment
Three quantitative metrics evaluate distinct aspects of model performance. Accuracy measures whether the final answer is mathematically correct, recorded as a binary value (0 or 1). Efficiency evaluates computational responsiveness and output economy using a weighted formula: Time Score (40%), Token Score (30%), and Conciseness Score (30%), with all sub-components bounded on a 0–1 scale. Consistency measures the degree of agreement among outputs produced by the same technique across multiple independent runs on the same problem, computed as the proportion of identical normalized outputs to total runs. Consistency is incremental and provisional: it is unavailable on the first run, at which point an explicit provisional flag is recorded; from the second run onward, consistency is computed and updated with each additional run.
Three-Tier Hierarchical Selection Algorithm
A hierarchical decision-making algorithm processes benchmarking results using a dual-mode execution framework to identify the most effective prompting technique for each problem.
Normal Mode uses Tier 1 and Tier 2 for efficient selection without executing all techniques. Benchmark Mode uses Tier 3 greedy runtime evaluation to generate performance data.
Tier 1 operates in Normal Mode at the highest level of specificity. The system builds a problem profile that includes domain, difficulty level, intent, mathematical features, structure, and constraints.
It compares the current problem with historical data using three similarity measures the lexical similarity using Jaccard coefficient, feature similarity using Jaccard coefficient, and embedding similarity using cosine similarity
These are combined using the formula:
Hybrid Similarity = 0.60 × Embedding + 0.25 × Feature + 0.15 × Lexical
A match is considered valid when it meets the minimum similarity threshold (0.55, escalating to 0.60 when more than 15 records in the database have a hybrid similarity score of at least 0.20 with the current problem), at least 3 samples per technique pass the filter, and the confidence gap between the top two candidates is at least 0.05. If any condition is not met, the system proceeds to Tier 2.
Tier 2 operates in Normal Mode when Tier 1 does not produce a confident result. The system groups historical data based on domain and difficulty level. A technique is selected when at least 5 evaluated samples per technique exist within the same domain–difficulty cell and the confidence gap between the top two candidates is at least 0.05. If confidence is still insufficient, the system moves to Tier 3 in Benchmark Mode.
Tier 3 operates in Benchmark Mode and serves as both the initial data generation step and the fallback when historical data is insufficient.
All prompting techniques are executed on the current problem. Each technique is evaluated using the formula:
Overall Score = 0.5 × Accuracy + 0.3 × Consistency + 0.2 × Efficiency
The technique with the highest score is selected.  If scores are equal, deterministic tie-breaking is applied using the SHA-256 hash of the normalized problem text string (lowercased, whitespace-stripped), with the technique assigned to the lower hash value in lexicographic comparison selected as the winner, ensuring reproducibility across sessions.
The results from this mode are stored and used to improve Tier 1 and Tier 2 decisions over time.
As more data is collected through Benchmark Mode, the system increasingly relies on Normal Mode for faster and more efficient selection. This design separates evaluation from deployment, allowing the system to maintain both accuracy and efficiency.
Output Component
The output component delivers three categories of actionable insights.
Performance Metrics
Comprehensive quantitative data reporting accuracy rates (percentage correct by technique, domain, and difficulty level); response times (mean and standard deviation in seconds); token usage (input and output length statistics); and consistency scores (proportion of identical normalized outputs per technique-problem pair, reported by domain and complexity level). Statistical significance testing through one-way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) at a significance level of p < 0.05 indicates which performance differences are meaningful rather than attributable to random variation.
Optimal Recommendations
Recommendations are based on empirical data generated in Benchmark Mode and applied through Normal Mode. When sufficient historical data is available, recommendations include confidence levels and expected performance ranges to indicate reliability.
Reusable Framework
A uniform evaluation methodology suitable for novel mathematical problems, additional prompting techniques, or alternative LLM models. The framework comprises: a fully documented dataset of 45 expert-validated problems with standardized formatting; validated evaluation procedures for all three performance metrics; a testing protocol documented to ensure reproducibility; and the three-tier hierarchical selection algorithm incorporating the three-layer hybrid similarity scoring mechanism at Tier 1 implemented as open-source software and published on GitHub. This reusability extends the study's impact beyond its immediate findings by enabling structured and systematic progress in prompt engineering research for mathematical reasoning.
Integration and Flow
The IPO model represents a unified system where inputs are processed through structured methods using a dual-mode execution design.
Benchmark Mode generates reliable performance data through greedy runtime evaluation. Normal Mode uses this data to make fast and efficient selections.
This approach addresses common issues in prompt engineering such as inconsistent evaluation methods, lack of standard benchmarks, and low reproducibility.
By introducing a structured and data-driven framework, the study transforms prompt engineering from a trial-and-error process into a systematic and reliable methodology.
2.3 Synthesis
The preceding literature review reveals three converging gaps that collectively motivate the design of this study. Prior research on prompting techniques, mathematical reasoning, evaluation methodology, and algorithmic optimization each provides partial insights, but no existing work integrates them into a unified, reproducible framework for mathematical problem-solving.
The first gap concerns evaluation adequacy. Brown et al. (2020) and Kojima et al. (2022) established the foundational effectiveness of few-shot and zero-shot prompting, but their evaluations target general NLP tasks rather than mathematical reasoning. Forootani (2025) and Stolfo et al. (2023) demonstrated that mathematical failures are predominantly structural and domain-specific, not linguistic—making general benchmarks and single-metric accuracy reporting systematically inadequate for this domain. Son (2025) and Wang et al. (2024) confirmed that existing evaluation frameworks are inconsistent and miss process-quality dimensions. The present study addresses this gap by implementing a three-metric weighted composite (accuracy, consistency, efficiency) across domain-stratified, complexity-stratified problem sets, producing evaluation data that prior frameworks cannot generate.
The second gap concerns benchmarking standards. Aqsa et al. (2025) demonstrated that general-purpose benchmarks produce unreliable cross-study comparisons in mathematical contexts, while Wang et al. (2025) and Ggaliwango et al. (2024) documented the broader reproducibility deficit in prompt engineering research. Existing mathematical benchmarks, including the MATH dataset used here (Hendrycks et al., 2021), provide high-quality source problems but are not paired with standardized evaluation protocols or domain-specific comparison frameworks. The present study addresses this by constructing a curated 45-problem benchmark with fixed normalization rules, documented few-shot examples, and open-source evaluation scripts—transforming a source dataset into a reproducible benchmarking instrument.
The third gap concerns algorithmic selection. Bachmann et al. (2024), Liu et al. (2024), and Sahoo et al. (2024) all concluded that no single technique dominates across problem types and difficulty levels, yet the field lacks any systematic mechanism for identifying which technique to use for a given problem. Existing selection approaches Bayesian search (Wang et al., 2025), metaheuristic frameworks (Kottapalli Narayanaswamy & Muniswamy, 2025) are computationally intensive and not designed for domain-specific mathematical deployment. Ramnath et al. (2025) confirmed that greedy approaches remain competitive in low-resource settings, validating the practical suitability of the algorithm developed here. The three-tier hierarchical selection algorithm directly fills this gap by providing a confidence-gated, data-driven mechanism that matches technique recommendations to problem characteristics using accumulated benchmarking evidence.
Where prior work treats benchmarking, evaluation, and selection as separate concerns or addresses none of them systematically this study integrates all three into a unified framework. The domain-specific benchmarking protocol addresses the evaluation adequacy gap identified by Aqsa et al. (2025) and Son (2025). The multi-metric composite addresses the process-quality gap identified by Wang et al. (2024) and Forootani (2025). The three-tier algorithm addresses the selection gap identified by Bachmann et al. (2024) and Liu et al. (2024). Together, these components transform prompt engineering for mathematical LLM applications from a manual, heuristic process into a systematic, evidence-based methodology, the central contribution of this study.


III. METHODOLOGY
This chapter describes the research design, model formulation, computational procedures, and evaluation methods used in the study. It explains how data were collected, processed, and analyzed to achieve the stated objectives.

3.1 Research Design
This section addresses Specific Objective 1 (comparing zero‑shot and few‑shot prompting) and Specific Objective 3 (evaluating the selection algorithm).

This research employed an experimental benchmarking design to systematically compare two prompting techniques across controlled mathematical problem-solving scenarios. Controlled experiments compared zero-shot and few-shot prompting across 45 problems spanning three mathematical domains and three complexity levels. The study evaluated performance differences using statistical analysis.
3.2 Model Formulation
This section addresses Specific Objective 2 (developing a prompt selection algorithm).
The selection model is grounded in Black-Box Optimization Theory (Li et al., 2025), which frames prompt selection as an empirical optimization challenge where internal model parameters remain hidden but outputs are measurable.




Figure 3.1 
Comparison of the Base Model and the Proposed Algorithmic Selection Optimization Model
       
The base model represents the conventional approach: a single technique is manually selected and applied to a problem, and outputs are evaluated using binary correctness alone. This approach is heuristic, subjective, and not reproducible across different problem types.
The proposed model introduces three enhancements over the base. First, both prompting techniques are evaluated systematically for every problem through controlled benchmarking. Second, a multidimensional evaluation protocol replaces binary scoring by assessing each response using three weighted metrics: Accuracy (50%), Consistency (30%), and Efficiency (20%). Third, a three-tier hierarchical selection algorithm is introduced within a dual-mode execution framework. The increased complexity of this framework over a simpler single-pass approach is explicitly justified by the study’s goal of enabling automatic, domain-sensitive technique selection a task requiring graduated evidence retrieval from problem-specific profiles to domain-level aggregation to real-time evaluation, none of which is achievable with a manual or single-pass approach.
3.2.1 Two‑Mode Selection Algorithm
This section addresses Specific Objective 2 in detail.
The algorithm answers one decision question: which technique should be used for the current mathematical problem, zero-shot or few-shot?
In practice, Normal Mode attempts selection through Tier 1 first, then Tier 2 if Tier 1 is not confident. Benchmark Mode (Tier 3) is triggered only when Normal Mode cannot make a confident recommendation.
The decision policy is confidence-gated and follows three tiers:
Step 1 (Tier 1 – Profile‑Based): The system first checks whether sufficiently similar historical problems exist using a hybrid similarity score: Hybrid Similarity = 0.60 × Embedding + 0.25 × Feature + 0.15 × Lexical (60%, 25%, and 15% weights, respectively). A profile match is considered decision-ready when the similarity threshold is met (0.55, or 0.60 under dense-candidate conditions), at least 3 records per technique are available, and the mean composite score gap between techniques is at least 0.05; if these conditions hold, the higher-scoring technique is selected.
Step 2 (Tier 2 – Domain‑Difficulty): If Tier 1 is not confident, the system aggregates historical results within the same domain and difficulty level. If at least 3 records per technique exist and the mean score difference ≥ 0.05, select the higher‑scoring technique. 
Step 3 (Tier 3 – Benchmark Mode / Greedy Runtime): If confidence is still insufficient, the system evaluates both techniques on the current problem, selects the higher-scoring technique, and stores the result for future Tier 1 and Tier 2 decisions.
Technique scoring uses the weighted composite metric:
Overall Score = 0.5 × Accuracy + 0.3 × Consistency + 0.2 × Efficiency  
When consistency is not yet available on the first run, a provisional score is used:
   Provisional Score = (0.5 × Accuracy + 0.2 × Efficiency) / 0.7 
 If scores are tied, deterministic tie-breaking is applied using SHA-256 of the normalized problem text to ensure reproducibility.  
Confidence decisions use minimum sample and score-gap thresholds defined for this study. Operationally, this design prioritizes fast evidence-based recommendations in Tiers 1 and 2, while Tier 3 preserves decision reliability when historical evidence is limited.
Detailed implementation parameters (similarity weighting, thresholds, tie-breaking, and code-level pseudocode) are documented in the implementation materials and reflected in Figure 3.2.
Pseudocode
FUNCTION select_technique(P):
   Normal Mode: Tier 1
    similar = historical[ hybrid_similarity(P, hist) >= 0.55 ]
    if len(similar) >= 3 per technique and score_gap(similar) >= 0.05:
        return best_technique(similar), tier=1
    Normal Mode: Tier 2
    cell = historical[ domain=P.domain and difficulty=P.difficulty ]
    if len(cell) >= 5 per technique and score_gap(cell) >= 0.05:
        return best_technique(cell), tier=2
    
Benchmark Mode: Tier 3 
    for technique in [zero_shot, few_shot]:
        run technique on P, compute overall_score
    winner = argmax(overall_scores)
    store_results(winner)
    return winner, tier=3

3.2.2 Why Not a Simpler Approach?
A simpler strategy was considered, such as assigning one fixed technique per domain-difficulty cell or evaluating both techniques on every new problem.
	This study adopts a three-tier selection policy because these simple alternatives fail to satisfy the study goals of per-problem reliability and runtime efficiency.
First, problem-level variation exists even within the same domain and difficulty. A fixed rule (for example, always use one technique for all intermediate algebra problems) can miss item-specific differences and produce avoidable errors.
Second, exhaustive runtime evaluation is inefficient. Running both techniques for every new problem increases latency and computational cost. The tiered policy reduces this cost by using historical evidence whenever confidence is sufficient.
Third, confidence-aware fallback is necessary for robust recommendations. When historical evidence is sparse or ambiguous, the policy escalates the decision to direct runtime comparison before storing the result for future use.
Accordingly, the three-tier structure is retained not to broaden the research scope, but to improve decision reliability within a narrow decision space: selecting between zero-shot and few-shot prompting.
Confidence criteria follow the minimum sample and score-gap thresholds defined in Section 3.2.1.
Empirical support for this design is presented in Section 4.3 through measured performance gains and runtime savings over baseline selection.

3.8 Evaluation Metrics
This section supports Specific Objective 1 by defining accuracy, consistency, and efficiency metrics.
Performance was assessed using three quantitative metrics weighted in the composite formula: 
Overall Score = (Accuracy × 0.5) + (Consistency × 0.3) + (Efficiency × 0.2).
Accuracy (weight: 50%). A binary measure (1 = correct, 0 = incorrect) comparing normalized model output against expert-verified solutions. The score is averaged across three runs per technique–problem combination.
Consistency (weight: 30%). Consistency measures the reliability of a technique’s outputs across repeated runs. After three runs, consistency is calculated as:
Consistency = Number of identical normalized outputs in 3 runs / 3
Interpretation: 1.00 = all three runs identical; 0.667 = two of three runs match; 0.00 = no two runs match.
The complete normalization procedure and incremental tracking (including the provisional flag on the first run) are described in Section 3.6.
Efficiency (weight: 20%).
Computed as:
Efficiency=(0.40×Time Score)+(0.30×Token Score)+(0.30×Conciseness Score)
All sub‑components are bounded on a 0–1 scale by design.
Time Score uses fixed latency tiers:
1.0 for 1–3 seconds, 0.8 for 4–6s, 0.6 for 7–10s, 0.5 for 11–20s, 0.4 for 21–30s, 0.3 for >30s.
Token Score averages a token count score (1.0 for ≤100 tokens, decreasing to 0.3 for >1,000) and a token‑to‑word ratio score (optimal range 1.0–2.0).
Conciseness Score assigns 1.0 for 30–100 words, 0.8 for 20–29 or 101–150 words, 0.6 for 10–19 or 151–250 words, and 0.5 for <10 or >250 words.
Provisional Score (first run only).
On the first run of a technique‑problem combination, consistency is not yet computable. For real‑time decisions (e.g., during Benchmark Mode Tier 3), a provisional overall score is used:
Provisional Score= Accuracy×0.5+Efficiency×0.2/0.7
This provisional score is flagged and replaced with the full composite score once three runs are completed. The final reported results in Chapter IV use only full composite scores.
The complete scoring implementation is published in the GitHub repository.
3.9 Benchmarking Framework
This section supports Specific Objective 1 (baseline methods) and Objective 3 (validation).
3.9.1 Baseline Methods
Zero-shot prompting served as the primary baseline the minimum-intervention approach requiring no example preparation. Few-shot prompting served as the enhanced comparison technique. These represent the foundational design spectrum of prompt engineering and are the most systematically comparable methods for rigorous evaluation (Brown et al., 2020; Kojima et al., 2022).
3.9.2 Benchmark Datasets and Test Instances
The 45-problem dataset was sourced from the MATH Dataset (HuggingFace), providing high-quality problems with documented difficulty levels and verified solutions. The dataset size aligns with recommendations from Aqsa et al. (2025) for domain-specific evaluation.
3.9.3 Performance Evaluation and Validation
Automated testing scripts executed the following workflow: JSON-formatted problems were loaded with metadata; zero-shot and few-shot techniques were applied to Llama 3; outputs were recorded per run; normalization was applied; and consistency was computed incrementally. All evaluation was performed under Benchmark Mode.
3.10 Statistical Validation
This section supports Specific Objective 1 by describing the statistical procedures used to compare zero-shot and few-shot prompting performance.
Primary statistical comparisons were conducted using one-way ANOVA atα=0.05α=0.05 to assess differences in composite scores between techniques across the benchmark. Domain-level and complexity-level ANOVA analyses were also performed to examine whether observed differences were consistent across problem groupings. Where applicable, post-hoc comparisons were used to identify specific group differences.
To improve interpretability, inferential results were evaluated together with descriptive statistics, including means, standard deviations, and confidence intervals across repeated runs. Practical significance (magnitude of observed differences) was considered alongside statistical significance in interpreting results.
Because each composite score includes a consistency component (30%) derived from the same repeated runs used to compute other metrics, partial internal dependency is present. Therefore, statistical findings are interpreted as benchmark-specific indicative evidence rather than fully independent population-level inference. Given the sample size (45 problems), conclusions are bounded to the evaluated domains, difficulty levels, and model configuration.
Overall, statistical validation in this study is used to strengthen comparative evidence within a controlled benchmark setting, not to claim universal generalization beyond the defined scope.

The most significant trade-off is between accuracy and efficiency. Few-shot prompting improves accuracy across complexity levels—with the strongest advantage at the advanced level—but requires more tokens and longer responses, increasing latency. Zero-shot remains a viable alternative for basic and intermediate problems where performance gaps are not statistically significant (p = 0.093 and p = 0.071).
A second trade-off involves consistency scoring reliability versus evaluation time. Three independent runs substantially improve reliability but triple evaluation duration. The provisional single-run scoring mechanism provides an intermediate option for deployment contexts where evaluation speed is prioritized.
A third trade-off concerns the behavior of the composite score under near-zero accuracy conditions. When both techniques consistently produce incorrect answers, the algorithm designates a winner based on consistency and efficiency alone. A technique that produces the same wrong answer across all three runs receives a consistency score of 1.00, inflating its composite score. Future implementations should flag problem profiles where the winning technique's accuracy is zero and apply a correctness-gating condition before storing recommendations in Normal Mode.
Overall, these trade-offs reinforce the value of an adaptive, data-driven selection framework. A static commitment to either technique across all problem types sacrifices either efficiency or accuracy unnecessarily. The three-tier algorithm addresses this by matching recommendations to problem characteristics and accumulated empirical evidence.





V. SUMMARY, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
5.1 Summary
This section summarizes findings for all three objectives.
This study investigated the performance of zero‑shot and few‑shot prompting techniques in solving mathematical problems using a controlled benchmark of 45 problems across algebra, pre‑calculus, and counting and probability.
A structured evaluation framework was implemented using three key metrics: accuracy (50%), consistency (30%), and efficiency (20%). Consistency was measured through repeated executions with normalized output comparison to ensure reproducibility, while efficiency evaluated response time and computational cost.
To address performance variability between techniques, the study introduced a prompt selection framework that recommends the more appropriate technique based on observed performance patterns. The system was evaluated in two configurations:
Baseline (best static technique per domain‑difficulty cell)
Full selection system (three‑tier algorithm with Normal Mode and Benchmark Mode)
Results show that few‑shot prompting improves accuracy, particularly in more complex problems, while zero‑shot prompting provides faster and more efficient responses for simpler tasks. The evaluation also demonstrated that the selection approach improves overall performance compared to using a single fixed technique. The full system matched the upper bound established by exhaustive greedy evaluation (running both techniques on every problem) on 100% of problems, while saving 80% of runtime evaluations.
Consistency analysis revealed that model outputs can remain stable across runs, but stability does not always guarantee correctness. This highlights the importance of combining consistency with accuracy in evaluation.
Overall, the study provides a structured and reproducible benchmark for comparing prompting techniques and demonstrates that data‑driven selection improves effectiveness within a controlled scope.
5.2 Conclusions
This section draws conclusions organized by each specific objective. This study had three objectives. The conclusions below answer each one.
Objective 1: Compare zero‑shot and few‑shot prompting.
What we found: Few‑shot prompting works better than zero‑shot on hard problems. On easy and medium problems, both work about the same.
For basic and intermediate problems: zero‑shot is fine. It is faster and uses fewer words.
For advanced problems: few‑shot is better. It gives more correct answers and more consistent outputs.
For counting and probability problems: neither technique worked well on harder questions. This topic is difficult for both methods.
Using three metrics (accuracy, consistency, efficiency) gave a better overall picture than just looking at correct answers. But when both techniques were always wrong, the algorithm still picked a winner based on consistency and speed — this is a weakness we note for future work.
Objective 2: Develop a selection algorithm.
What we did: We built a three‑tier selection algorithm that chooses the better prompting technique for a given problem.
The algorithm has two modes:
Normal Mode: Uses past results to recommend a technique without running both on a new problem.
Benchmark Mode: Runs both techniques, compares them, and saves the results.
The algorithm only recommends when it is confident (enough past data and a clear score difference). If not confident, it runs both techniques. This saves time while keeping accuracy.
Objective 3: Test the algorithm against baseline selection.
What we found: The full three‑tier selection system significantly outperformed the best static baseline and matched the upper bound established by exhaustive greedy evaluation.
Baseline: average composite score 0.7628
Full selection system: average composite score 0.8262
Improvement: +0.0634 (+8.31%), statistically significant (p = 0.0156)
The full system matched the greedy‑only upper bound on all 45 problems (100% match rate). This means the algorithm can reliably pick the better technique using only past data, without running both techniques on every new problem. In Normal Mode, 80% of problems were resolved at Tier 1 or Tier 2, saving approximately 4 seconds per problem compared to exhaustive evaluation.
Within the limits of this study, we conclude:
Use few‑shot for advanced problems. Use zero‑shot for simpler ones — it is faster and still accurate.
The three‑tier selection algorithm works. It saves time without losing quality.
The algorithm performs better than always using the same fixed technique.
This study offers a simple, repeatable way to choose between two prompting methods for math problems. The same approach can be used for more techniques and larger problem sets in the future.
5.3 Recommendations
This section provides future work recommendations based on all three objectives.
Limitation Acknowledgment: Findings are limited to controlled benchmark conditions (45 problems, three domains, Llama 3) and may not generalize to broader mathematical problem distributions.
Based on the findings and limitations of this controlled benchmark study, the following recommendations are proposed for researchers, educators, and practitioners working with prompt engineering for mathematical problem‑solving using large language models.
Expand Prompting TechniquesFuture studies should include additional prompting strategies beyond zero-shot and few-shot, such as:
Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting
Role-based prompting (e.g., framing the model as a tutor)
Self-consistency methods (e.g., majority-vote sampling)
Incorporating these techniques will allow broader evaluation of prompt engineering strategies and strengthen claims about performance improvements.
Increase Dataset Size and DiversityThe current dataset of 45 problems is limited in size and scope. Future research should:
Expand datasets to include more problems per domain and difficulty level
Incorporate diverse problem types, such as word problems, geometry, proofs, and real-world applications
This expansion will improve statistical validity, enhance generalizability, and enable more robust evaluation of selection strategies.
Validate Across Multiple ModelsSince this study used only one AI model, future work should:
Test the selection approach across multiple large language models
Compare differences in accuracy, consistency, and performance
This ensures that findings are not specific to a single model and increases confidence in broader applicability.
Improve Algorithm EvaluationFurther validation of the selection mechanism can be achieved by:
Testing alternative selection strategies (e.g., rule-based, learning-based)
Comparing with more advanced optimization or hybrid approaches
This strengthens the contribution of the selection framework and provides insight into its practical utility.
Enhance ReproducibilityFuture studies should:
Standardize normalization and equivalence rules for evaluating model outputs
Provide publicly available datasets, evaluation scripts, and example implementations
These measures support transparency, allow independent verification, and facilitate adoption by other researchers.
Focus on Real-World ApplicationFinally, the practical utility of the selection approach should be evaluated in real educational settings by:
Deploying it in tutoring systems or automated problem-solving tools
Collecting human-rated solution quality and longitudinal learning outcome data
This will demonstrate whether adaptive selection strategies improve actual learning outcomes compared to fixed prompting approaches.
